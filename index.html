Master Index Workbench

Pick the big areas, mix them, add or drop any measure — and watch the ranking rebuild live. Every approximation stays visible. Smaller than conventional indices — and able to prove every number they can't.

Why trust this? Methods & choices ↓
Sourced data. Every value shown is real, sourced and dated (see the provenance block in dataset.js). Blanks are honest gaps — where no verifiable figure exists, nothing is shown. The default view is the gold tier: measures that measure what their name says, from the best available source. The optional extended tier adds known-compromised measures — clearly badged — so you can see what conventional-index shortcuts do to a ranking.

Methods & choices

Why this exists

City indices keep multiplying — liveability, green, smart, competitive — until the labels stop meaning anything. This workbench takes the opposite bet: one honest engine, one shared pool of measures, and you decide what to blend. Its only claim to authority is that every number can prove where it came from.

The rules we hold ourselves to

  • No number is ever invented, estimated or interpolated. If a value can't be verified from a real source, the cell stays blank — and the blank is explained (click it).
  • Every value carries its receipt. Source, reference year, extraction method and caveats are recorded per value and shown in the click-through popover. Reference years differ across cities within a measure; we show the year rather than pretend otherwise.
  • Compromises are flagged, never hidden. Metro-level stand-ins, country proxies, crowd-sourced and modelled figures are all badged in the table, and the amber line above the ranking counts them.
  • Missing data costs visibility, not rank. A city missing a measure has that measure dropped from its own score and its coverage % reduced — it is never punished with an assumed zero.

The two tiers

Every sourced measure sits in one of two tiers. The gold tier (29 measures, the default view) contains only measures that measure what their name says, from the best available source. The extended tier (6 measures, off by default behind the labelled toggle) contains measures we judged compromised and say so: an OECD-only economy pair that blanks every non-OECD city, a Europe-only knowledge-worker share, a water statistic mixing 2016–2022 vintages, an open-data score that measures national statistics offices rather than cities, and one measure — Wikidata museum counts — included purely as a bias demonstration (Paris counts at 1.8× its official museums, Shanghai at 0.4×, London near zero from a database-linkage artifact; the numbers are reproducible, and reproducibly wrong).

Why include them at all? Because conventional indices are built from exactly this kind of data — silently. Here you can switch it on, watch the ranking move, and see every compromised cell badged and explained. The remaining 3 catalog measures are permanently blank: no honest source exists for them, and the reasons are recorded. That is the difference between a small index and a dishonest one.

These rules are enforced by machinery, not just promised. Every proposed change to the dataset must pass an automated integrity check before it can be published: it rejects any value that lacks a matching provenance record, any blank without an explanation, any invalid honesty flag, and any drift between the app's data and the downloadable CSV. Every run of the check is public — including the failures. The maintainers cannot quietly break these rules; the repository would refuse.

How big should this get?

We are at 55 cities and 35 sourced measures — which was the target, and roughly where we intend to stay. The reasoning:

  • Cities. Below ~30, the 0–100 rescaling is twitchy — one outlier stretches everyone's scale. Around 50, we cover essentially every city people actually compare, while staying inside the geography where verifiable public sources exist. At 100+, honesty breaks: new cells would be mostly country proxies and blanks, trading integrity for the appearance of breadth. (GPCI, the most methodologically serious comparable index, covers 48 cities — not a coincidence.)
  • Measures. Below ~4 per area, one bad source can swing an entire area score. Above ~40 in total, look-alike measures (income, GDP, venture capital…) start silently over-weighting a single idea, and every extra measure is another source to keep fresh, forever. Established indices sit in the same band: EIU ~30, Mercer 39.
  • The bar that matters is not the counts but: at least 85% of cells filled, 100% of filled cells with provenance, and every blank explained. We would rather be the reference for 50 cities than a rumour about 200.

Why so small? How this compares

Conventional indices cover 100–200 cities and dozens of indicators. This workbench is deliberately smaller — not because rigour is easier at this size, but because we refuse to add a cell that can't meet the bar. The honest comparison:

Conventional indices are genuinely better at breadth, long time series, and expert judgement where hard data does not exist. If you need 200 cities, use them. If you need to check the receipts — and to see how much a ranking wobbles when a measure is dropped — you're in the right place.

Known limits

Reference years are mixed within measures (each value shows its own). Some cities carry state- or province-level stand-ins where nothing finer is published. A few sources are crowd-maintained or modelled and are flagged as such. Definitions genuinely differ for waste recycling across cities — the caveats say so bluntly. When a better source appears, the value is upgraded and the provenance updated.

Data & reuse

Download: dataset.csv (one row per city × measure, with reference year, source and notes) · dataset.js (full provenance, machine-readable). This is version 1.0.0 (released 2026-07-12); versions are tagged on GitHub, so citations stay stable while the data evolves. Adding cities or measures re-scales all scores, so scores are comparable within a version — not across versions, and not between the gold and extended views.
