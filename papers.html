A city index that shows its receipts: radical provenance, tiered honesty, and machine-enforced integrity in composite urban indicators

POON King Wang · full working paper · describes artifact v3.0.0 · July 2026 · artifact: kr48vr.github.io/master-of-all-indices

Abstract. Composite city indices — liveability, sustainability, smartness, competitiveness — shape investment, migration and policy, yet almost none allow a reader to verify a single underlying number. We present the Master Index Workbench, a 55-city, 35-measure composite-index instrument designed around a discipline we call radical provenance: every value carries a click-through receipt (source, reference year, extraction method, caveats); every missing value is a documented blank rather than an estimate; and both properties are enforced by public, automated integrity checks that reject any violating change — including values outside declared plausibility bounds. Two further mechanisms extend the discipline. A two-tier taxonomy separates measures that measure what their name says (gold, n=29) from knowingly compromised measures (extended, n=6) that users can activate to observe, live, what conventional-index data shortcuts do to a ranking — including a deliberately retained crowd-data measure whose biases we quantify (Paris registers 1.8× its official museum count; Shanghai 0.4×; London near zero through a database-linkage artifact). A freshness contract compares each measure's last-verified date against its source's publication cadence and publishes the verdict monthly. Construction surfaced three findings: the geography of verifiability is systematically skewed against non-OECD cities; crowd-maintained data measures editing activity rather than urban reality; and honest blanks constitute an informative catalogue of failures in the global urban-statistics system. We argue that receipts, tiers, machine-enforced rules and freshness contracts are a transferable, inexpensive template for honest composite indicators.

1. Introduction: the index genre and its verification gap

Composite city indices have become a governance genre. The Economist Intelligence Unit ranks liveability across 173 cities; Mercer ranks quality of living across 241; IMD ranks "smartness" across 148; the Mori Memorial Foundation's Global Power City Index ranks 48 cities on "magnetism"; and consultancies publish annual green, digital, creative, resilient and talent rankings whose league tables circulate far beyond their methodological annexes. Their influence is material: rankings anchor location decisions, justify infrastructure budgets, calibrate expatriate compensation, and script political narratives of urban success and decline (Kitchin, Lauriault & McArdle 2015; Acuto, Steenmans & Iaione 2021).

The methodological critique of composite indicators is mature. Weighting and aggregation choices are consequential and contestable; uncertainty and sensitivity analysis should accompany any league table (Saisana, Saltelli & Tarantola 2005); the OECD–JRC Handbook (2008) codified construction practice; and a substantial literature has catalogued the "issues of weighting, aggregation and robustness" that composite indices inherit by construction (Greco et al. 2019). A parallel literature critiques the data politics of urban measurement: indicators embed silent judgements about what counts and who counts it (Kitchin et al. 2015), and platformised data pipelines obscure provenance further (Barns 2020).

Yet this critique has chiefly improved producer guidance, not reader affordances. A reader confronting a published city rank still cannot answer four elementary questions:

  1. Where does this number come from? Published indices cite source families in annexes; none, to our knowledge, exposes per-value provenance — the specific document, reference year and extraction path behind each cell.
  2. How old is it? Reference years are typically homogenised or undisclosed; staleness is invisible.
  3. What was compromised to obtain it? Country-level values presented at city level, modelled estimates, perception surveys and crowd sources are blended without per-cell disclosure — and missing data is routinely imputed, invisibly (the EIU, for example, explicitly substitutes analyst judgement where data are absent).
  4. What would the ranking look like under my priorities? Publisher weights are fixed; the reader cannot re-derive the league table under a different mandate, and a published rank cannot be reproduced from public materials.

This paper presents an instrument built so that all four questions are answerable by construction: the Master Index Workbench. Its contributions are: (i) a provenance-first data model and interface in which every value carries a click-through receipt and every blank an explanation; (ii) a machine-enforcement layer — public continuous-integration checks that reject violations of the honesty rules, including plausibility-bound violations; (iii) a two-tier taxonomy that quarantines knowingly compromised measures behind an explicit control, converting data-quality critique into a live, user-operable demonstration; (iv) a freshness contract publishing monthly staleness verdicts; and (v) three empirical findings about the global urban-statistics system that the honesty machinery itself surfaced. The cost of the discipline is breadth — 55 cities rather than 200 — and we argue this is the correct trade for decision-grade use.

2. Related work and positioning

Three literatures frame the contribution. Composite-indicator methodology (Saisana et al. 2005; OECD/JRC 2008; Becker et al. 2017; Greco et al. 2019) established that weighting, normalisation and aggregation choices materially move ranks, and prescribed sensitivity analysis; the Workbench operationalises this prescription as a user-facing control (live reweighting, method switching, and a drop-one-measure stability read-out) rather than a producer-side annex. Critical data studies of urban indicators (Kitchin et al. 2015; Barns 2020; Mörtenböck & Mooshammer 2021) diagnosed opacity and performativity in city benchmarking; the Workbench responds with an existence proof that radical transparency is implementable at product quality. Open-science infrastructure — FAIR data principles (Wilkinson et al. 2016), data provenance standards such as W3C PROV (Moreau & Groth 2013), archived citation (CITATION.cff; versioned releases) — supplies the machinery vocabulary; the Workbench applies it to a genre, consumer-facing composite indices, where it has been conspicuously absent.

The nearest antecedents are official-statistics dashboards with per-series metadata (e.g., OECD regional statistics), which document at series level but not at cell level, do not quarantine compromised measures, and do not expose user-reproducible league tables; and open-data indices (ODIN; Open Data Barometer), which assess statistical systems but are themselves published without per-value receipts. We are not aware of a prior composite index that publishes per-value provenance, machine-enforces its own honesty rules in public, or ships its rejected data as an interactive exhibit.

3. Design principles

Four rules govern the dataset; each is enforced by machinery rather than editorial promise.

  1. No number is ever invented, estimated or interpolated. A value that cannot be verified from a real source is recorded as null, and the null carries a per-city explanation. The rule is absolute: it excludes analyst judgement, interpolation between years, imputation from similar cities, and combination of published components under assumed weights (e.g., Hong Kong's life expectancy remains blank because its statistics office publishes sex-disaggregated figures only; any combined figure would smuggle in a weighting assumption).
  2. Every value carries its receipt. The data model stores, per value: source (document-level, not family-level), reference year, extraction method, and caveats — rendered in the interface as a click-through popover on every cell.
  3. Compromises are flagged, never hidden. Per-measure honesty flags (data level: city / metro / country proxy; approximation: none / crowd / perception / self-reported / modelled / proxy; direction; caveat) drive visible badges; per-value notes carry cell-specific compromises; and values whose reference year lags four or more years behind their measure's median wear a visible year-chip.
  4. Missing data costs visibility, not rank. A missing measure is dropped from that city's own aggregation and lowers its displayed coverage percentage; it is never imputed as zero or as a peer mean.

The enforcement layer is a public continuous-integration check that rejects any proposed dataset change containing: a value without a matching provenance record; a provenance record disagreeing with its stored value; a blank without an explanation; an invalid flag or tier; a missing cell (a sourced measure must carry an explicit value-or-explained-blank for every city); drift between the application's data and the downloadable CSV; or a value outside its measure's declared plausibility envelope, so that a transcription error (759 for 75.9) fails loudly even when fully provenanced. Every check run, including failures, is public. A parallel monthly job enforces the freshness contract (§5.4).

4. Data and construction

4.1 Catalog and roster

A 38-measure catalog spans six areas — Liveable, Green, Smart, Competitive, Talent, Connected — of which 35 are sourced at v3.0.0 (Table 1) and three are permanently blank because no honest source exists (renewable energy share at city level; net working-age talent migration at metro level; travel time to global hubs). The roster grew from a 16-city pilot to 55 cities in two waves under an explicit admission rule: a city was admissible if it projected at most three honest blanks across the then-sourced 19 measures, with five named exceptions (Dubai, Shanghai, Beijing, Shenzhen, Delhi) admitted on the argument that their gaps are structural properties of source coverage rather than informational poverty, and that excluding the world's most-compared cities would misrepresent the urban world more than their blanks misrepresent them.

Post-sourcing actuals diverged from projection — nine additional cities breached the floor because five sources proved structurally thin outside the OECD (§6.1) — and the divergence is documented, with the full projected-versus-actual table, in the public roster file rather than silently reconciled. The subsequent measure-expansion phase explicitly prioritised globally covered sources in response.

4.2 Sourcing and extraction practice

Values were extracted from primary publications wherever they exist: national statistical yearbooks, communiqués and census bulletins (including Chinese municipal statistics, each carrying the caveat that independent verification is not possible); multilateral datasets (UNODC crime statistics; WHO ambient air quality and health workforce; ITU 5G population coverage; UNESCO researcher density; UN-Habitat open-space access; UN e-government LOSI; World Bank Worldwide Governance Indicators); official open data (Ookla broadband performance tiles aggregated over documented bounding boxes; OECD SDMX metropolitan and functional-urban-area tables; Eurostat regional series; the EC Joint Research Centre's EDGAR emissions on GHS urban centres); commercial-but-published lists (Forbes Global 2000 headquarters, aggregated under the official OMB 2023 combined-statistical-area delineations); survey products flagged as perception (IMD Smart City Index statement scores); and crowd sources flagged as crowd (Numbeo edition tables pinned to byte-verified archival URLs; Wikipedia airline-destination tables audited against revision history; Wikidata counts under a fixed uniform query).

Extraction was performed by AI research agents under a strict rule set — no value from a search snippet or from model memory; every value transcribed from a document fetched during the session; raw evidence archived — followed by human-visible integration in per-measure commits. Verification practices are catalogued exhaustively in the companion QA ledger; representative examples: the CO2 pipeline reproduces the JRC's own published per-capita field exactly for all 55 cities before our derived values were accepted; IMD survey scores parsed programmatically were spot-checked visually against ten rendered PDF city profiles (10/10 matches); Numbeo values cite pinned edition URLs verified byte-identical to live tables at extraction time; ThinkHazard administrative divisions were breadcrumb-verified after naive name-matching was caught assigning eight cities to wrong-country namesakes; Wikipedia airport tables were audited against revision history, yielding three principled blanks where citation purges had gutted tables mid-project.

4.3 The two-tier taxonomy

Every catalog measure carries a tier: gold — the measure measures what its name says, from the best available source (29 measures; the default view); extended — knowingly compromised, shipped off-by-default behind a labelled control (6 measures); or none — permanently blank, no honest source exists (3). The extended tier's six members, with their specific compromises: OECD-only labour productivity (33/55 cities, and structurally absent for Australian, Canadian and Israeli metros) and employment growth (37/55, mixed 5-year windows); Europe-only knowledge-intensive employment (23/55); household water use from the only surviving multi-city compilation (34/55, vintages 2016–2022); national statistics-openness (ODIN) standing in, with disclosed concept drift, for defunct city-level open-data indices; and Wikidata museum counts retained purely as a quantified bias demonstration (§6.2). Activating the tier re-scores the index, badges every extended measure, and marks the interface (a persistent banner and a ranking-header tag) so a screenshot cannot pass an extended ranking off as the gold one.

Tier assignments and every accept/reject decision were governed by an explicit decision log (eleven recorded decisions, D1–D11, including two human overrides of AI recommendations), published with the sourcing verdicts for all nineteen candidate measures examined in the expansion phase — including, for each rejected measure, the specific sources evaluated and the reasons no honest fill exists.

5. The instrument

5.1 Scoring

For the active measure set M and displayed city set C, each measure is min–max rescaled to 0–100 across C, inverted where lower is better, and aggregated measure → sub-theme → area → composite under user weights:

scorec,m = 100 · (xc,m − minC xm) / (maxC xm − minC xm)  [inverted if direction = lower]
compositec = Σa wa · areac,a / Σa wa  (additive)  or  Πa areac,awa/Σw  (geometric)

Missing values are dropped from the city's own aggregation (rule 4), reducing displayed coverage. Because rescaling depends on both the city set and the active measure set, scores are comparable within a version and tier view only; the citation guidance requires stating both. Equal area weights are the default; the interface exposes weights, per-measure toggles and the aggregation switch directly.

5.2 Robustness affordance

A stability control re-computes the index once per active measure, dropping one measure each run, and displays each city's best and worst rank across runs — the reader-facing form of the sensitivity analysis the methodology literature prescribes for producers. Narrow ranges identify robust positions; wide ranges identify rankings that hinge on a single measure.

5.3 Reproducible sharing

Any configuration — areas, weights, individual measures, aggregation method, tier state — serialises into a URL as a compact diff from the gold default. Opening the link reproduces the exact ranking; a shared result is thereby a recipe rather than a screenshot. Every published claim about the instrument, including the figures in this paper, is reproducible by a reader in one click.

5.4 The freshness contract

Each measure declares its source's publication cadence with reasoning (Numbeo publishes semiannual editions; the ITU drops data each July and December; IMD publishes each March; Forbes each June; unversioned sources are re-probed twice yearly). A monthly automated job compares every measure's last-verified date against its declared cadence, classifies it fresh / due soon / overdue, appends a standing watchlist of non-age risks (a pending upstream data revision; a known forthcoming national 5G launch; two source-license confirmations), and files the report as a public issue. The instrument's masthead displays the latest verdict live. At the time of writing: 35/35 measures fresh; the mechanism was validated by a simulated run eight months forward, which correctly flagged all eleven fast-cadence measures overdue.

6. Results

At v3.0.0 the dataset holds 1,702 provenanced values of 1,925 possible cells (88.4% coverage); all 223 blanks are explained; 42 values (2.5%) wear staleness chips; 100% of present values carry receipts. Three construction findings are results in their own right.

6.1 The geography of verifiability is skewed

An index that refuses to estimate renders source geography visible. Five candidate measures were quarantined or rejected because their best available sources structurally exclude non-OECD cities: OECD metropolitan GDP and labour series (zero coverage of our fifteen non-OECD cities; no productivity series at all for Australian, Canadian or Israeli metros), Eurostat knowledge-intensive employment (Europe-only), and startup-ecosystem counts (absent for smaller non-Western hubs). Two blueprint sources died during construction (the Open Data Barometer's last edition is 2018; the IBNET water utility database's domain no longer resolves) and one became login-gated (the WCCD ISO 37120 portal). The practical consequence: the fifteen non-OECD cities of our roster are systematically harder to describe honestly — after full sourcing, nine non-exception cities exceeded the roster's three-blank floor, every excess blank traceable to five OECD- or Europe-bound sources. Conventional indices covering such cities at parity with Copenhagen are, necessarily, estimating — and not saying where.

6.2 Crowd data measures editing activity, not the city

The extended tier retains one measure as a deliberate, quantified bias demonstration: museums per 100,000 residents, counted from Wikidata under a single uniform, reproducible rule (instance-of museum or subclass; located-in the city's administrative territory; the city item's own population claim as denominator). Against official registers, Paris counts 235 museums versus ~130 officially recorded (1.8× overcount); Shanghai counts 65 versus 165–171 officially registered (0.4× undercount) — a relative bias of roughly 4.5× between two of the world's premier museum cities, attributable to editorial attention rather than cultural infrastructure. A second, structural bias compounds it: London (8 counted), Amsterdam (1), Copenhagen (0) and Auckland (0) score at or near zero because their institutions' location chains terminate at graph entities other than the conventional city item — the British Museum's chain reaches "Greater London" but never "London". The values are reproducible, and reproducibly wrong; activating them visibly re-ranks the index (Copenhagen falls from third to fifth; Figure 2 in the executive brief renders the shift). We suggest this pattern — shipping a rejected measure as a labelled exhibit — as a general device for indicator literacy.

6.3 Honest blanks are an informative catalogue

Refusing to estimate produced 223 documented absences whose reasons are themselves data about the urban-statistics system: sex-only life tables (Hong Kong); a last-published recycling rate from 2013 that credits source-reduction — waste never generated — and was abandoned by its own jurisdiction (Los Angeles); a metropolitan statistical agency publishing tonnages but no rates (Massachusetts); a genuine 0.0% (Türkiye's 5G population coverage through 2024, recorded as true with a scheduled re-check); crowd tables gutted by citation purges mid-project (Tokyo, Osaka and Taipei airline destinations); and three catalog concepts with no honest source anywhere. Each blank names a specific, checkable failure of measurement infrastructure — a research agenda in negative space.

7. Discussion

For index producers, the Workbench demonstrates that per-value receipts, explained blanks, quarantined compromises and published freshness are implementable at consumer quality with negligible infrastructure — the entire instrument is a static page and two dependency-free scripts; the enforcement layer is free public CI. The genre's opacity is therefore now a choice, not a constraint. For index consumers — governments benchmarking themselves, firms siting investments, journalists citing ranks — the four reader questions of §1 double as procurement criteria, and the extended tier supplies the due-diligence question for any vendor: which of your indicators would sit in this tier, and why isn't that disclosed? For the measurement community, the findings of §6 argue that honesty machinery is itself an instrument of discovery: the skew of verifiability, the crowd-bias magnitudes, and the catalogue of absences were not design goals but by-products of refusing to estimate.

The template generalises beyond cities: national composites, corporate ESG scores and university rankings share the same verification gap and could adopt the same four mechanisms — receipts per value, tiers for compromise, machine-enforced rules, freshness contracts — essentially unchanged.

8. Limitations

  1. Source concentration: four measures draw on one crowd provider (Numbeo), correlating their errors; the flags disclose this but cannot remove it.
  2. Proxy grain: several measures serve city-level questions with country- or region-level values (flagged per measure and per value); the differential distortion is largest precisely where sub-national statistics are weakest.
  3. Definitional heterogeneity: waste recycling mixes genuinely different national definitions (recycling, separate collection, diversion), disclosed bluntly per value rather than harmonised.
  4. Snapshot, not series: the instrument is a versioned current snapshot; conventional indices remain superior for longitudinal analysis.
  5. Single-team extraction: though evidence-archived, adversarially reviewed (six independent verifier passes on the expansion plan) and machine-checked, extraction was performed by one team; the deterministic extraction rules and shareable configurations are published precisely to invite independent re-derivation.
  6. License inheritance: the compilation carries non-commercial terms inherited from upstream sources; two upstream license confirmations remain on the public watchlist.

9. Conclusion

The credibility problem of composite indices is not that they simplify — all measurement simplifies — but that they conceal: sources, ages, compromises and the sensitivity of their verdicts. The Master Index Workbench is an existence proof that a composite index can show its receipts, wear its best-before dates, quarantine its own compromises as a teaching exhibit, and submit its honesty to public machine enforcement — at the deliberate cost of breadth. Fifty-five cities with receipts, we argue, serve decisions better than two hundred without; and the mechanisms that make it so are cheap, general, and now demonstrated.

The index you can interrogate

An illustrated executive brief for public- and private-sector leaders · 6-minute read

You already use city indices. They appear in your board papers, your location decisions, your talent strategies, your budget narratives and your press coverage. And you have almost certainly never been able to verify a single number in one — where it came from, how old it is, or what was compromised to produce it. When a ranking moves against you, you cannot audit it; when it moves for you, you cannot defend it beyond the headline.

The Master Index Workbench is built for exactly that gap: a 55-city index across liveability, green, smart, competitive, talent and connectivity where every number shows its receipt — click any value and see its source, reference year, extraction method and caveats — and every missing number explains itself instead of being quietly estimated. The rules are enforced by public automated checks, not editorial promises: a value without a receipt, or outside sanity bounds, is mechanically rejected before it can be published. Data ages in the open, too — every measure's freshness is checked monthly against its source's publication rhythm, and the report is public.

What one click buys you

What it lets you do that no conventional index can

  • Rebuild the ranking to your mandate. An investment committee weighing connectivity, a ministry weighing liveability, a relocation team weighing cost — each can reweight areas, drop measures, and switch aggregation, with the ranking rebuilding live. The publisher's priorities stop masquerading as facts.
  • Share decisions as recipes, not screenshots. Any configuration serialises into a link that reproduces the exact ranking. A ranking you circulate to your board is auditable by your board.
  • See what data shortcuts actually change. A deliberately quarantined "extended tier" contains the kinds of compromised measures conventional indices blend in silently — wrong geographies, decade-old vintages, crowd data with demonstrated bias. Switch it on and watch the ranking move (the figure below shows exactly that experiment). It is a live tutorial in why index literacy matters, and a due-diligence question generator for any index a vendor sells you: which of your indicators would sit in this tier?
  • Trust the blanks. Where you see no number, it is because no honest number exists — and the blank says why. In an era of confident dashboards, a documented "we don't know" is a competitive information advantage.

The experiment your current index never lets you run

Why the tiers exist

What it is honestly not

It covers 55 major cities, not 200; it is a current snapshot, not a time series; and where hard data does not exist it offers a blank, not expert judgement. Conventional indices are genuinely better at breadth and history. Use them for reach — and use this instrument to learn what questions to ask of them.

Three uses this quarter

  1. Benchmark with receipts: put your city's (or your candidate cities') profile in front of your team with every number's provenance one click away — and reweight it under your strategy's priorities rather than a publisher's.
  2. Stress-test a narrative: before citing any ranking publicly, check whether the claim survives a change of weights, a dropped measure, or the stability check (which shows how far each city's rank moves when any single measure is removed).
  3. Raise your procurement bar: adopt the four workbench rules — receipts, explained blanks, flagged compromises, published freshness — as evaluation criteria for any data or index product you buy.

The strategic point. Indices govern quietly: they set default comparisons, anchor negotiations, and write headlines. The organisations that thrive under measurement are those that can interrogate the measures. This instrument exists to make that interrogation ordinary — start at the Workbench, read Methods & choices, and make a blend of your own.

King & AI

How a human and an AI built an honest index together — the contract, the full task-level ledger, and the three modes of working

31pull requests (AI-opened) 29merges (King-reviewed) 90commits authored by AI 3releases published by King ~45background agents dispatched by AI 19formal decisions ruled by King

The collaboration ran on a simple contract, set by King on day one: non-negotiable rules (never invent a number; never hide a compromise) plus staged gates (plan first, approval before data, King merges every pull request, King publishes every release). Within that contract the AI operated with wide autonomy — and the contract is why the autonomy was safe.

Every interaction in this record is tagged with one of three modes, using King's own taxonomy: tool — King directed, the AI executed faithfully to spec; teammate — the outcome was shaped by both, through proposal, pushback, or override; independent — one of us (or neither of us: the machines) worked alone, meeting the other at a gate.

Of the 53 steps: 24 teammate — the mode of every decision, every design exchange, and every override; 14 tool — the mode of specification-driven building and data fills; 15 independent — the mode of agent-fleet research, adversarial self-review, King's solo gates (merges, releases, live testing), and the two automated routines that now need neither of us. The pattern is not "human asks, machine does": the decisive steps were almost all teammate-mode, the heavy lifting tool-mode, and the scale independent-mode — with the gates ensuring every independent stretch ended in human review.

The three modes, distilled

King used AI as a tool — precise instruction, faithful execution

  • "I have switched the environment network access. Please fetch everything." → the AI extracted and provenanced the entire pilot dataset to the pre-agreed plan.
  • "Do the provenance UI." → the click-through receipt popover, built to the existing design language.
  • "Let's do 1 → 3 → 2." → plausibility bounds, staleness chips and shareable blends, delivered in the ordered sequence with tests.

King and AI as teammates — ideas transformed by passing between us

  • The skyline (steps 38–42). King proposed a city-skyline visual; the AI argued a literal skyline was the genre's cliché and counter-proposed a skyline made of the data; King accepted, then improved it twice — the looping blend-tour animation, and the amber-when-yours colour link to the Share button; the AI then contributed the tempo judgment (2.6s frames) and the honesty details (hover-pause, real blends only). Neither of us would have designed the final artifact alone.
  • The extended tier (step 24). The AI recommended skipping compromised measures; King reframed them as a product: quarantine them behind a toggle so users can watch what conventional shortcuts change. That reframing became the project's sharpest argument.
  • Decision overrides (step 23). On D8, King chose the wider CSA metro rule against the AI's recommendation — accepting its tradeoffs knowingly; the AI then enforced the rule uniformly and documented the consequences. Disagreement, resolution, execution: the teammate loop working as intended.

We worked independently — parallel work, meeting at gates

  • While King slept or worked elsewhere, the AI ran research-agent fleets (waste-recycling statistics across 40 cities; national statistics for 13 Asian cities; six adversarial verifiers auditing the Phase-3 plan) and integrated results into reviewed pull requests.
  • King, alone, merged 29 pull requests, published 3 releases, and tested the live site — finding four real bugs no automated test had caught, each reported with a screenshot and each fixed the same session.
  • And some work now happens with neither of us (steps 52–53): the integrity gate rejects bad data on every push, and the freshness robot files its public report every month, whether or not either of us is watching.

What the record suggests

The division of labour was consistent: King owned values, decisions, taste and verification-in-the-wild — the rules, every tradeoff ruling, every design direction, every merge and release, and the four bugs only a real user finds. The AI owned execution at scale and rigor under the rules — extraction, engineering, testing, adversarial self-review, and the discipline of writing every compromise down. The gates made the autonomy trustworthy; the machinery made the trust checkable; and the best ideas — the tier system, the data skyline — belonged to neither of us alone.
