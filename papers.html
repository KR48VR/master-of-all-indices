A city index that shows its receipts: radical provenance, tiered honesty, and machine-enforced integrity in composite urban indicators

POON King Wang · working paper, v3.0.0 · July 2026 · artifact: kr48vr.github.io/master-of-all-indices

Abstract. Composite city indices — liveability, sustainability, smartness, competitiveness — shape investment, migration and policy, yet almost none allow a reader to verify a single underlying number. We present the Master Index Workbench, a 55-city, 35-measure composite-index instrument designed around a discipline we call radical provenance: every value carries a click-through receipt (source, reference year, extraction method, caveats); every missing value is a documented blank rather than an estimate; and both properties are enforced by public, automated integrity checks that reject any change violating them — including values outside declared plausibility bounds. Two further mechanisms extend the discipline. A two-tier taxonomy separates measures that measure what their name says (gold, 29) from knowingly compromised measures (extended, 6) that users can activate to observe, live, what conventional-index data shortcuts do to a ranking — including a deliberately included crowd-data measure whose biases we quantify (Paris registers 1.8× its official museum count; Shanghai 0.4×; London near zero through a database-linkage artifact). A freshness contract compares each measure's last-verified date against its source's publication cadence and publishes the result monthly. We describe the construction, quantify what the extended tier reveals, and argue that receipts, tiers and machine-enforced rules constitute a transferable template for honest composite indicators.

1. The problem: indices without receipts

City indices have proliferated into a genre. The Economist Intelligence Unit ranks liveability; Mercer ranks quality of living; IMD ranks smartness; the Mori Memorial Foundation's Global Power City Index ranks "magnetism"; dozens of consultancies rank green, digital, creative and resilient cities. The methodological critique of such composites is mature: weighting and aggregation choices are consequential and contestable (Saisana, Saltelli & Tarantola 2005; OECD/JRC 2008; Greco et al. 2019), and the data pipelines feeding urban dashboards embed silent judgements (Kitchin, Lauriault & McArdle 2015). But the critique has mostly produced better guidance for index builders, not better verification affordances for index readers. In practice a reader confronting a published city rank cannot answer four elementary questions: Where does this number come from? How old is it? What was compromised to obtain it? And what would the ranking look like under my priorities rather than the publisher's?

The Workbench is an existence proof that all four questions can be answered by construction, at the cost of breadth — 55 cities rather than 200 — and we argue that this trade is the honest one for decision-grade use.

2. Design principles

Four rules govern the dataset, and each is enforced by machinery rather than editorial promise (Methods & choices on the live instrument):

  1. No number is invented, estimated or interpolated. A value that cannot be verified from a real source is recorded as a blank, and the blank carries its explanation (e.g., Hong Kong's life expectancy is blank because its statistics office publishes sex-disaggregated figures only, and combining them would require a weighting assumption — i.e., estimation).
  2. Every value carries its receipt. Source, reference year, extraction method and caveats are stored per value and rendered as a click-through popover.
  3. Compromises are flagged, never hidden. Metro-level stand-ins, country proxies, crowd-sourced, perception-based, self-reported and modelled figures are badged; values whose reference year lags 4+ years behind their measure's median wear a visible year-chip.
  4. Missing data costs visibility, not rank. A missing measure is dropped from that city's own score and reduces its displayed coverage; it is never imputed as zero.

The enforcement layer is a public continuous-integration check that rejects any proposed change containing a value without a matching provenance record, a blank without an explanation, an invalid honesty flag, a missing cell, drift between the application's data and the downloadable CSV, or a value outside its measure's declared plausibility envelope — so a transcription error (759 for 75.9) fails loudly even when fully provenanced. Every run, including failures, is public.

3. Construction

Catalog and roster. A 38-measure catalog spans six areas (Liveable, Green, Smart, Competitive, Talent, Connected). The city roster grew from a 16-city pilot to 55 cities under an explicit admission rule (at most three honest blanks of the 19 then-sourced measures, with five named exceptions admitted for structural reasons). Reality diverged from projection: after sourcing, nine additional cities breached the floor because five sources proved structurally thin outside the OECD. Rather than silently relax the rule, the divergence is documented in the public roster file, and the subsequent expansion phase explicitly prioritised globally covered sources.

Sourcing and extraction. Values were extracted from primary publications wherever possible: national statistical yearbooks and communiqués (including Chinese municipal sources, recorded with the caveat that independent verification is not possible), UN and multilateral datasets (UNODC, WHO, ITU, UNESCO, UN-Habitat, World Bank WGI), official open data (Ookla, OECD SDMX, Eurostat, EDGAR/GHSL), and — where flagged as crowd or perception — Numbeo tables pinned to byte-verified edition URLs and survey products (IMD Smart City Index). Where official websites blocked automated access, archived copies of the official documents were used and disclosed per value. Verification practices included reproducing the publisher's own arithmetic (our EDGAR urban-centre CO2 per-capita pipeline reproduces the JRC's published per-capita field exactly for all 55 cities) and visual spot-checking of parsed values against rendered source documents.

Tier taxonomy. Each measure is assigned gold (measures what its name says, from the best available source; 29), extended (knowingly compromised — OECD-only coverage, Europe-only coverage, mixed vintages, concept drift, or demonstrated bias; 6), or none (no honest source exists; 3, permanently blank with reasons recorded). The extended tier ships off by default behind a labelled control; activating it re-scores the index and marks the interface so a screenshot cannot pass an extended ranking off as the gold one. Tier assignment decisions, including the rejection rationale for every candidate source examined, are recorded in a public decisions file (PHASE3.md).

Scoring. For the active measure set, each measure is min-max rescaled to 0–100 across the displayed cities, lower-is-better measures are inverted, and scores roll up measure → sub-theme → area → composite under user-controlled area weights, with additive and geometric aggregation available. Because rescaling depends on both the city set and the active measure set, scores are comparable within a version and tier view only — a warning carried in the citation guidance. A stability control re-computes the index once per active measure, dropping one each time, and displays each city's rank range. Any configuration — areas, weights, measures, method, tier — serialises into a shareable URL that reproduces the exact ranking, making every shared result a recipe rather than a screenshot.

Freshness. Each measure declares its source's publication cadence (Numbeo semiannual editions; ITU July/December drops; IMD each March; Forbes each June; unversioned sources re-probed twice yearly). A monthly automated job compares every measure's last-verified date against its cadence and files the report as a public issue; the instrument's masthead displays the latest verdict live. Staleness is thereby a visible property of the dataset rather than a quiet one.

4. What the honesty machinery reveals

At v3.0.0 the dataset holds 1,702 provenanced values of 1,925 possible cells (88.4%); all 223 blanks are explained; 42 values (2.5%) wear staleness chips. Three findings are worth reporting as results in their own right.

The geography of verifiability is skewed. Five candidate measures were rejected or quarantined because their best available sources structurally exclude non-OECD cities (metro GDP and labour statistics, knowledge-intensive employment) or have decayed (two blueprint sources died during construction; a third became login-gated). An index that refuses to estimate makes this skew visible: the fifteen non-OECD cities in our roster are systematically harder to describe honestly, and conventional indices covering them at parity are, necessarily, estimating.

Crowd data measures editing activity, not the city. Our deliberately included bias demonstration counts museums per 100,000 residents from a crowd-maintained knowledge graph under one uniform, reproducible rule. Against official registers, Paris is overcounted 1.8× while Shanghai is undercounted to 0.4× — a relative bias of roughly 4.5× between two of the world's premier museum cities — and London, Amsterdam, Copenhagen and Auckland score at or near zero purely because their institutions link to different graph entities than the conventional city item. The numbers are reproducible, and reproducibly wrong; activating them visibly reshuffles the ranking (Copenhagen exits the top three).

Honest blanks are informative. Refusing to estimate produced a catalogue of instructive absences: no honest combined life expectancy exists for Hong Kong; no official recycling rate exists for Los Angeles more recent than a 2013 diversion figure that credits waste never generated; Türkiye's 5G coverage was genuinely 0.0% through 2024 and is recorded as such; three catalog measures (renewable share, talent inflow, travel time to global hubs) have no honest source at all. Each blank documents a specific failure of the global urban-statistics system.

5. Discussion and limitations

The Workbench trades breadth for verifiability, and several limitations follow. Four measures depend on one crowd provider (Numbeo), correlating their errors. Country proxies serve city-level questions for some measures, flagged but coarse. Waste recycling mixes genuinely different definitions across cities, disclosed bluntly per value. The compilation license inherits non-commercial terms from upstream sources. Extraction, though evidence-archived and adversarially reviewed, was performed by a single team; independent re-derivation is invited — the published extraction rules are deterministic, and the shareable-blend mechanism makes any published claim reproducible by a reader in one click.

The transferable claim is architectural: receipts per value, tiers for compromise, machine-enforced rules, and published freshness contracts are general mechanisms, applicable to any composite indicator — national, corporate ESG, university rankings — and cheap to operate (the entire instrument is a static page and two dependency-free scripts). The genre's credibility problem is not that composite indices simplify; it is that they conceal. Concealment, this project suggests, is now a choice.

The index you can interrogate

An executive brief for public- and private-sector leaders · 4-minute read

You already use city indices. They appear in your board papers, your location decisions, your talent strategies, your budget narratives and your press coverage. And you have almost certainly never been able to verify a single number in one — where it came from, how old it is, or what was compromised to produce it. When a ranking moves against you, you cannot audit it; when it moves for you, you cannot defend it beyond the headline.

The Master Index Workbench is built for exactly that gap: a 55-city index across liveability, green, smart, competitive, talent and connectivity where every number shows its receipt — click any value and see its source, reference year, extraction method and caveats — and every missing number explains itself instead of being quietly estimated. The rules are enforced by public automated checks, not editorial promises: a value without a receipt, or outside sanity bounds, is mechanically rejected before it can be published. Data ages in the open, too — every measure's freshness is checked monthly against its source's publication rhythm, and the report is public.

What it lets you do that no conventional index can

  • Rebuild the ranking to your mandate. An investment committee weighing connectivity, a ministry weighing liveability, a relocation team weighing cost — each can reweight areas, drop measures, and switch aggregation, with the ranking rebuilding live. The publisher's priorities stop masquerading as facts.
  • Share decisions as recipes, not screenshots. Any configuration serialises into a link that reproduces the exact ranking. A ranking you circulate to your board is auditable by your board.
  • See what data shortcuts actually change. A deliberately quarantined "extended tier" contains the kinds of compromised measures conventional indices blend in silently — wrong geographies, decade-old vintages, crowd data with demonstrated bias. Switch it on and watch the ranking move. It is a live tutorial in why index literacy matters, and a due-diligence question generator for any index a vendor sells you: which of your indicators would sit in this tier?
  • Trust the blanks. Where you see no number, it is because no honest number exists — and the blank says why. In an era of confident dashboards, a documented "we don't know" is a competitive information advantage.

What it is honestly not

It covers 55 major cities, not 200; it is a current snapshot, not a time series; and where hard data does not exist it offers a blank, not expert judgement. Conventional indices are genuinely better at breadth and history. Use them for reach — and use this instrument to learn what questions to ask of them.

Three uses this quarter

  1. Benchmark with receipts: put your city's (or your candidate cities') profile in front of your team with every number's provenance one click away — and reweight it under your strategy's priorities rather than a publisher's.
  2. Stress-test a narrative: before citing any ranking publicly, check whether the claim survives a change of weights, a dropped measure, or the stability check (which shows how far each city's rank moves when any single measure is removed).
  3. Raise your procurement bar: adopt the four workbench rules — receipts, explained blanks, flagged compromises, published freshness — as evaluation criteria for any data or index product you buy.

The strategic point. Indices govern quietly: they set default comparisons, anchor negotiations, and write headlines. The organisations that thrive under measurement are those that can interrogate the measures. This instrument exists to make that interrogation ordinary — start at the Workbench, read Methods & choices, and make a blend of your own.

The quality ledger

Everything done to hold content, data, rigor, visuals and design to the highest standard — with the incidents that proved the system works

1. Data integrity, enforced mechanically

  • The integrity checker (scripts/check-integrity.js, run by CI on every push and pull request) rejects: values without matching provenance; provenance disagreeing with stored values; blanks without explanations; invalid honesty flags or tiers; missing data cells; values outside per-measure plausibility bounds; CSV drift from the source data.
  • Plausibility bounds with negative tests: every sourced measure declares a sane envelope (definitional bounds exact: 0–100 indices, 0–3 hazard coding; physical bounds generous: life expectancy 55–95). Verified by deliberately planting a typo (759 for 75.9) — CI failed naming the exact cell — and by deleting a bounds entry — CI failed demanding it.
  • An incident that hardened the system: during the Phase-3 audit we found one measure (price-to-income) had never been filled for 13 cities — the cells were missing rather than blank, which evaded the explained-blank rule. The gap was filled the same day and the checker extended so a missing cell is now itself a CI failure. The failure mode is extinct.

2. Source verification practices

  • Fetch-verified or it doesn't count: no value was recorded from a search snippet or memory; every value was transcribed from a document fetched during the session, with raw evidence archived. Where official sites blocked automation, archived copies of the official documents were used and disclosed per value.
  • Reproducing the publisher's own arithmetic: the CO2 pipeline (EDGAR emissions ÷ urban-centre population) was validated by reproducing the JRC's own published per-capita field exactly, for all 55 cities, before our values were accepted.
  • Pinned editions: Numbeo values cite pinned edition URLs verified byte-identical to the live tables at extraction time.
  • Rendered-document spot checks: IMD survey scores were parsed programmatically, then 10 cities were read visually from the rendered PDF — all matched. Forbes HQ data had every correction hand-logged (a "Stockhold" typo; tax-domicile vs operational headquarters calls), and US metro assignment used the official OMB 2023 delineations, not intuition.
  • Crowd-source forensics: Wikipedia airport tables were audited against revision history; three cities (Tokyo, Osaka, Taipei) are honest blanks because citation purges gutted their tables — including a purge detected mid-project that changed our answer. ThinkHazard divisions were breadcrumb-verified after naive matching was caught assigning eight cities to wrong-country namesakes (Amsterdam to Suriname; Bangkok to a single canal district).

3. Adversarial verification

  • The Phase-3 sourcing plan was audited by six independent verifier agents, each cross-checking the plan's claims against archived evidence. They surfaced ~25 findings that were then fixed — among them: a wrong "world #1" claim (Abu Dhabi is second to Ajman on Numbeo safety); an evidence file that proved only a 50/55 first-pass match for the CO2 join; a hazard class misdescribed (Bangkok's river flood is Medium; the High classes are coastal and heat); a mis-filed section placing a recommended measure under "Rejected"; and headline arithmetic that didn't yet reflect two approved decisions.
  • Every wave of data integration ended with a plausibility read of the full ranking before commit — the step that caught, e.g., a transit measure briefly reading 24,400% from a units artifact.

4. Honest-blank governance

  • Principled exclusions were preferred over publishable-but-misleading numbers: Los Angeles' 2013 diversion figure (credits waste never generated) was quoted in the blank's explanation rather than recorded; Australia's 2010 researcher density was blanked rather than passed off as current; Hong Kong's life expectancy stayed blank rather than combine sex-specific figures by assumption; Türkiye's genuine 0.0% 5G was recorded as true, with a refresh note.

5. Freshness as a watched property

  • Every measure declares its source's publication cadence with reasoning; a monthly workflow files the fresh/due/overdue verdict as a public issue; the instrument's masthead displays the latest verdict live; values 4+ years behind their measure's median wear a visible year-chip. Time-travel tested: a simulated run eight months ahead correctly flagged all eleven fast-moving measures overdue.

6. Visual and interaction quality

  • Playwright verification accompanied every UI change: 55 rows rendering, both aggregation methods re-ranking, coverage lines updating, tier toggle cycling cleanly, shareable-blend round-trips reproducing rankings in fresh pages, responsive behavior checked at 1440/1280/1000 widths, reduced-motion honoured, zero console errors as a merge condition.
  • Live-user testing found what lab testing missed — four real bugs, all fixed within the session they were reported: a CSS rule silently defeating the banner's hidden state; a toggle that revealed measures without scoring them; shared links failing in already-open tabs (fragment navigation doesn't reload); tour tooltips vanishing on repaint (now: hovering pauses the tour). The lab tests read attributes; the user used a browser. Both are now part of the definition of done.
  • Design honesty: the skyline's stretched heights are disclosed in the flags legend; the extended view marks the interface so screenshots can't misrepresent the gold ranking; even decoration obeys the no-randomness rule (silhouettes are deterministic hashes of city names).

7. Documentation that cannot drift far

  • Site statistics are computed live from the dataset (they cannot go stale); prose is audited against features — three catch-up passes were run after review questions, syncing the flags legend, Known limits, and the README's comparison table. Versioning follows semver with explicit comparability warnings (across versions, and between tiers); releases are tagged; CITATION.cff tracks each release.

The three modes, with real examples

King used AI as a tool — precise instruction, faithful execution

  • "I have switched the environment network access. Please fetch everything." → the AI extracted and provenanced the entire pilot dataset to the pre-agreed plan.
  • "Do the provenance UI." → the click-through receipt popover, built to the existing design language.
  • "Let's do 1 → 3 → 2." → plausibility bounds, staleness chips and shareable blends, delivered in the ordered sequence with tests.

King and AI as teammates — ideas transformed by passing between us

  • The skyline. King proposed a city-skyline visual; the AI argued a literal skyline was the genre's cliché and counter-proposed a skyline made of the data; King accepted, then improved it twice — the looping blend-tour animation, and the amber-when-yours colour link to the Share button; the AI then contributed the tempo judgment (2.6s frames, not 640ms) and the honesty details (hover-pause, real blends only). Neither of us would have designed the final artifact alone.
  • The extended tier. The AI recommended skipping compromised measures; King reframed them as a product: quarantine them behind a toggle so users can watch what conventional shortcuts change. That reframing became the project's sharpest argument.
  • Decision overrides. On D8, King chose the wider CSA metro rule against the AI's recommendation ("merge SF with San Jose and DC with Baltimore") — accepting its tradeoffs knowingly; the AI then enforced the rule uniformly and documented the consequences. Disagreement, resolution, execution: the teammate loop working as intended.

We worked independently — parallel work, meeting at gates

  • While King slept or worked elsewhere, the AI ran research-agent fleets (waste-recycling statistics across 40 cities; national statistics for 13 Asian cities; six adversarial verifiers auditing the Phase-3 plan) and integrated results into reviewed pull requests.
  • King, alone, merged 29 pull requests, published 3 releases, and tested the live site — finding four real bugs no automated test had caught, each reported with a screenshot and each fixed the same session.
  • And some work now happens with neither of us: the integrity gate rejects bad data on every push, and the freshness robot files its public report on the first of every month, whether or not either of us is watching.

What the record suggests

The division of labour was consistent: King owned values, decisions, taste and verification-in-the-wild — the rules, every tradeoff ruling, every design direction, every merge and release, and the four bugs only a real user finds. The AI owned execution at scale and rigor under the rules — extraction, engineering, testing, adversarial self-review, and the discipline of writing every compromise down. The gates made the autonomy trustworthy; the machinery made the trust checkable; and the best ideas — the tier system, the data skyline — belonged to neither of us alone. That, more than any single feature, is the finding of this tab.
